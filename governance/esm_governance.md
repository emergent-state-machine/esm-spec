# Emergent State Machine Governance Framework

*Version: v0.1.0 draft*  
*Date: 2026-03-16*

> Draft status: This governance framework is an early proposal and is open to community feedback.


## 1. Purpose

This document defines governance principles for systems implemented using the Emergent State Machine (ESM) architecture.

While the ESM specification defines the deterministic computational structure of the system, governance defines how domain experts design, approve, and maintain the policies that guide system behavior.

Governance ensures that ESM systems remain:

- safe
- transparent
- auditable
- aligned with domain expertise

This document applies to organizations deploying ESM systems in domains such as healthcare, education, industrial operations, and public services.

Because every decision cycle in an ESM produces a recorded Turn, governance is not limited to defining rules in advance; it also includes reviewing accumulated decision records and refining the signals, roles, and policies that guide future decisions.

## 2. Governance Philosophy

The ESM architecture separates computation from judgment.

The machine performs deterministic reasoning over defined signals and policies. Human authorities remain responsible for defining:

- signals
- policies
- playbooks
- acceptable system behavior

This separation ensures that the system remains interpretable while allowing human expertise to guide its operation.

Governance therefore focuses on how domain knowledge becomes encoded into system policy.

## 3. Governance Roles

Effective governance requires clearly defined roles.

Typical governance structures may include the following responsibilities.

### Domain Authorities

Domain authorities define and approve the signals used by an ESM.

Examples include:

- physicians defining clinical signals
- engineers defining manufacturing signals
- educators defining learning signals

Domain authorities ensure that signal definitions reflect current knowledge and practice.

### Policy Authorities

Policy authorities define the decision rules used by the ESM.

Responsibilities include:

- defining policy thresholds
- specifying intervention triggers
- approving policy updates

Policy authorities ensure that system decisions align with operational standards and safety requirements.

### Playbook Designers

Playbook designers create structured intervention strategies that guide system responses over time.

Responsibilities include:

- defining playbook steps
- specifying transition conditions
- determining escalation procedures

Playbooks translate policy goals into operational workflows.

### System Operators

Operators interact with the ESM during normal operation.

Responsibilities may include:

- reviewing system alerts
- selecting breadcrumb transitions
- initiating interventions

Operators remain responsible for exercising professional judgment when interacting with the system.

### System Auditors

Auditors review system behavior and recorded trajectories to ensure that the system operates as intended.

Responsibilities include:

- reviewing policy outcomes
- detecting unintended behaviors
- recommending policy revisions

Auditors help maintain system integrity over time.

## 4. Signal Governance

Signals represent the foundational quantities used to construct system state.

Because signals determine how the system interprets observations, signal definitions must be governed carefully.

Governance procedures should ensure that:

- signal definitions are approved by domain authorities
- derived signals are mathematically defined and inspectable
- signals correspond to meaningful domain concepts
- signal definitions are documented and versioned

Signal governance prevents uncontrolled drift in how system state is interpreted.

## 5. Policy Governance

Policies determine how the system responds to system state.

Policy governance ensures that decision rules remain aligned with domain standards.

Governance procedures should include:

- documented policy definitions
- approval procedures for policy changes
- version tracking for policy revisions
- safety review for high-impact policy updates

Policy updates should be informed by analysis of recorded system trajectories.

## 6. Playbook Governance

Playbooks define structured intervention strategies guiding system behavior over time.

Governance procedures should ensure that:

- playbooks reflect accepted operational practices
- escalation steps are clearly defined
- completion criteria are explicit
- playbook changes undergo review before deployment

Playbooks should be designed to guide the system toward desirable regions of state space while preserving operational safety.

## 7. Breadcrumb Governance

Breadcrumbs represent user-facing transition options generated by playbooks.

Governance procedures should ensure that:

- breadcrumb options are clearly interpretable by users
- breadcrumb sets correspond to valid transitions
- breadcrumb selections are logged

Breadcrumb design should support human decision-making without introducing ambiguity or unsafe options.

## 8. Policy Evolution

Over time, system behavior should be improved through analysis of recorded trajectories.

Policy evolution should follow structured processes that include:

- trajectory analysis
- expert review
- controlled deployment of policy updates

Changes to signals, policies, or playbooks should be documented and versioned.

## 9. Transparency and Auditability

Governed ESM systems must maintain complete traceability of system reasoning.

For every action taken by the system, it must be possible to reconstruct:

- the triggering policy rule
- the evaluated state vector
- the signals used
- the underlying observations

This traceability enables accountability and continuous improvement.

## 10. Multi-ESM Governance

Systems composed of multiple ESMs must maintain governance structures that define:

- how ESM handoffs occur
- how responsibilities are divided between ESMs
- how cross-ESM policies are coordinated

This ensures that modular ESM architectures remain coherent at system scale.

## 11. Risk Classification

ESM policies and playbooks may produce actions with varying levels of operational impact. Governance frameworks should therefore classify system policies according to their potential risk.

Risk classification helps determine:

- the level of expert review required
- the approval process for policy changes
- monitoring and auditing requirements

Risk levels may vary by domain but typically fall into three categories.

### 11.1 Low-Risk Policies

Low-risk policies produce actions that provide guidance or informational support without directly altering critical system behavior.

Examples include:

- educational prompts for students
- informational alerts
- non-critical monitoring notifications
- advisory workflow suggestions

Low-risk policies typically require standard review and may be updated through routine governance procedures.

### 11.2 Moderate-Risk Policies

Moderate-risk policies influence operational workflows or recommend interventions but do not directly execute irreversible or safety-critical actions.

Examples include:

- recommending diagnostic tests
- adjusting monitoring frequency
- suggesting workflow escalations
- triggering instructional interventions

Moderate-risk policies should undergo structured review and testing before deployment.

Governance procedures should include validation of policy thresholds and evaluation of expected system trajectories.

### 11.3 High-Risk Policies

High-risk policies involve actions that could significantly affect safety, health outcomes, or critical system operations.

Examples include:

- triggering medical intervention protocols
- initiating system shutdowns
- enforcing regulatory compliance actions
- applying high-stakes educational consequences

High-risk policies require the highest level of governance oversight.

Approval procedures should include:

- domain authority review
- safety validation
- formal documentation
- audit readiness

High-risk policies should be monitored closely after deployment to ensure correct system behavior.

### 11.4 Risk Review Procedures

Organizations deploying ESM systems should establish review procedures appropriate to each risk level.

Typical review practices include:

- policy documentation
- peer review by domain authorities
- simulation or test evaluation
- staged deployment

These procedures ensure that system policies remain aligned with operational safety and professional standards.

### 11.5 Risk Reclassification

Policies may change risk classification over time as systems evolve or operational contexts change.

Governance frameworks should therefore support periodic review of policy classifications.

Risk reclassification may be triggered by:

- changes in operational scope
- new domain knowledge
- observed system behavior
- regulatory requirements

Maintaining accurate risk classification helps ensure that governance oversight remains appropriate for the system's real-world impact.

## 12. Deployment Oversight

Before an ESM system is introduced into operational use, governance bodies should establish structured deployment procedures.

Deployment oversight ensures that system policies, signals, and playbooks have been validated before the system influences real-world workflows.

Deployment procedures should include the following steps.

### System Review

The ESM architecture and configuration should be reviewed to confirm that:

- signals are approved by domain authorities
- policies are documented
- playbooks reflect accepted operational practices
- risk classifications are assigned appropriately

### Validation Testing

Prior to deployment, the system should be evaluated using test scenarios or historical data.

Validation testing should verify that:

- state vectors are constructed correctly
- policy rules fire under expected conditions
- playbooks produce appropriate action sequences
- undesirable behaviors are not observed

### Staged Deployment

Where possible, deployment should proceed through staged phases such as:

- simulation or offline evaluation
- shadow mode monitoring
- limited operational deployment
- full operational deployment

Staged deployment allows organizations to identify unexpected behaviors before the system operates at full scale.

### Operational Approval

Final deployment approval should be granted by the appropriate governance authority once validation procedures are complete.

## 13. Continuous Learning and Improvement

ESM systems are designed to support ongoing refinement through analysis of recorded system trajectories.

Because each system decision remains traceable, domain experts can examine how system states evolved over time and determine whether policies or playbooks should be adjusted.

Governance frameworks should therefore include procedures for periodic system review.

Typical review activities include:

- analysis of state trajectories
- evaluation of policy outcomes
- identification of recurring system patterns
- review of operator interactions and breadcrumb selections

Insights gained from these analyses may inform improvements to:

- signal definitions
- policy thresholds
- playbook structures

Policy revisions should follow established governance procedures and undergo appropriate review before deployment.

This process enables gradual system improvement while maintaining transparency and safety.
